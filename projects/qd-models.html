Model Breakdown

Optimization — Staffing, Cost, & Assignment

Three problems that use queueing theory, linear programming, and binary integer programming to find the minimum-cost or minimum-time solution subject to real-world constraints.

Problem A — Checkout Staffing (M/M/c Queueing)

How many cashiers to meet the 5-minute wait target?

A retail manager's complaint about long lines gets quantified through an M/M/c queue. Arrival rates are Poisson, service times exponential — the model computes utilization, queue length, and expected wait for one, two, and three cashiers.

ρ   = λ / (c · μ)        -- server utilization
Lq  ≈ function(λ, μ, c)  -- expected number in queue
Wq  = Lq / λ             -- expected wait time in queue
W   = Wq + 1/μ           -- total time in system

Choose smallest c such that Wq ≤ 5 minutes.

One cashier: unacceptable wait times. Two: better but still misses target during peaks. Three: wait drops under five minutes and queue probability collapses — justifying the added labor cost.

Arrival rates, service rates, and queueing parameters driving checkout system performance

Problem D — Copier Selection (Linear Programming)

Which copier has the lower total daily operating cost?

A law office comparing a regular vs high-speed copier needed a structured cost model, not intuition. Total daily cost is a linear function of pages, copy rate, lease cost, and operating cost per hour.

hours_A      = V / rate_A
hours_B      = V / rate_B
TotalCost_A  = hours_A · cost_hrA
TotalCost_B  = hours_B · cost_hrB

Choose the copier with the lower TotalCost.

The high-speed copier finished the daily workload in fewer hours, saving roughly $11/day. Over a multi-year lease, that compounds into meaningful labor and operating cost savings.

Cost comparison — high-speed copier delivers lower combined operating and labor cost

Problem F — Machinist Assignment (Binary Integer Programming)

Which machinist goes to which machine to minimize total time?

Four machinists, four machines, different processing times per pairing, and one certification restriction. Classic assignment problem solved in LINGO with binary variables.

Minimize   Σᵢ Σⱼ ( time[i,j] · x[i,j] )

Subject to:
  Σⱼ x[i,j] = 1      each machinist gets one machine
  Σᵢ x[i,j] = 1      each machine gets one machinist
  x[3,turning] = 0   machinist 3 not certified
  x[i,j] ∈ {0,1}

Optimal: Machinist 1 → turning, Machinist 2 → milling, Machinist 3 → metal lathe, Machinist 4 → radial drill. Total time: 100 minutes — lower than any assignment the manager had tried.

LINGO solution — optimal machinist-machine pairing and 100-minute total processing time

Problem C — New Product Profit (Monte Carlo)

What's the distribution of profit per unit under uncertain costs?

A firm evaluating a new product knows the selling price but not its costs — purchase, labor, and transportation all vary. Instead of one point estimate, management needed the full distribution of profit outcomes so they could see both upside and downside.

For each trial i:
  purchase_cost[i]  ~ Dist_purchase(...)
  labor_cost[i]     ~ Dist_labor(...)
  transport_cost[i] ~ Dist_transport(...)

  profit_per_unit[i] = SellingPrice
                       - purchase_cost[i]
                       - labor_cost[i]
                       - transport_cost[i]

After 20 trials:
  μ  = mean(profit_per_unit)    ≈ $6.90
  σ  = sd(profit_per_unit)      ≈ $2.27
  CI = μ ± 1.96 · σ / √n        -- 95% CI: ~$4.50 to ~$9.00

What the simulation shows

  • • Average profit: ~$6.90 per unit
  • • Standard deviation: ~$2.27 — meaningful variability
  • • 95% confidence interval: roughly $4.50 to $9.00
  • • Even the low end of the CI is profitable — product is viable
  • • Simulation makes the downside visible, not just the expected case

Simulation trials — mean profit, variability, and confidence interval for the new product

Decision Models — Inventory & Legal Strategy

Two problems where uncertainty and payoffs are structured into tables and trees — giving managers a transparent, defensible way to choose instead of relying on gut instinct.

Problem B — Specialty Steak Stocking (Payoff & Regret Tables)

How many pounds to order each week for a perishable product?

Ordering too little leaves profit uncaptured. Ordering too much wastes margin. A grocery manager's intuition gets replaced by expected value, optimistic, pessimistic, and minimax regret criteria — all from the same structured payoff table.

Expected Profit(Q) = Σ_d [ P(D=d) · Profit(Q, d) ]

Regret(Q, d) = BestProfit(d) - Profit(Q, d)

Optimal: Q* that maximizes Expected Profit
         and performs well under minimax regret.

Ordering ~35 pounds per week produced the highest expected profit (~$79) and held up well across all four decision criteria — turning a gut-level stocking guess into a documented policy.

Problem E — Patent Litigation Strategy (Decision Tree & EMV)

Settle or go to trial? Expected monetary value of each path.

A patent infringement dispute with two options: accept a settlement or proceed to trial. Each path has different probabilities and very different financial outcomes. A decision tree makes the comparison transparent.

EMV(Settle) = Payoff_settlement

EMV(Trial)  = P(win)     · Payoff_win
            + P(lose)    · Payoff_lose
            + P(partial) · Payoff_partial

Choose the option with the higher EMV,
then weigh against the firm's risk tolerance.

Trial EMV: ~$1.8M. Settlement EMV: ~$1.5M. Financially, litigation creates more expected value — but the decision tree also makes the variance of each path visible, which matters for a risk-averse firm.

What I Learned

Key takeaways

  • • Queueing models turn vague "long lines" complaints into specific cashier counts — the math replaces the argument
  • • LP and BILP reveal cost and time savings that are genuinely non-obvious from intuition, especially with constraints
  • • Monte Carlo simulation is more useful than a point estimate when both upside and downside risk matter to the decision maker
  • • Payoff and regret tables give perishable inventory decisions a documented, auditable basis — not just a gut call
  • • Decision trees make the variance of each path visible, not just the expected value — which is often what matters most
