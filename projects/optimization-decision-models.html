Operations Management Optimization Simulation Decision Modeling

Operations Optimization & Decision Models

A six-part operations and supply chain project applying linear programming (LP), mixed-integer and binary models, queueing theory, decision trees, and Monte Carlo simulation to real-world scenarios in retail, legal services, and manufacturing. The work focuses on resource allocation, risk-aware decision-making, and cost-efficient process design.

Resource Optimization Quantitative Decision-Making

Project Narrative

As part of an Operations and Supply Chain Management course, this project tackled six structured business problems ranging from staffing cashiers and stocking perishable goods to evaluating legal strategy and assigning machinists. Each problem was modeled quantitatively, solved using tools like Excel Solver and LINGO, and translated into a concrete recommendation.

The result is a compact portfolio of operations research techniques showing how LP, Monte Carlo simulation, decision trees, and binary integer programming directly support profitability, service quality, and risk management.

Problem A – Customer Checkout Optimization

Queueing model to reduce balking and excessive waiting in a retail environment.

  • Objective: Determine the minimum number of cashiers required so customers rarely wait more than 5 minutes.
  • Methodology: Modeled arrivals as a Poisson process and service times as exponential (M/M/c queue), then evaluated utilization and waiting-time metrics for 1, 2, and 3 cashiers.
  • Result: At least three cashiers are required to keep waiting times under the target threshold and minimize customer balking.
ρ = λ / (cμ)              -- Utilization with c cashiers
P(wait > 5) ≈ P(queue > 0)·e^{-(μc-λ)·5}

Evaluate c = 1, 2, 3 and select the smallest c
that satisfies P(wait > 5 minutes) ≤ target.

Problem B – Specialty Steak Stocking

Payoff, regret, and sensitivity analysis for perishable inventory decisions.

  • Objective: Choose a weekly stocking level that maximizes expected profit while controlling risk of over- or under-stocking.
  • Methodology: Built payoff and regret tables for multiple demand levels, then applied expected value, optimistic, and pessimistic criteria, followed by sensitivity analysis.
  • Result: An order quantity of 35 pounds produced the highest expected weekly profit (~$79) and remained optimal under reasonable demand fluctuations.
Expected Value(Q) = Σ [ P(demand = d) · Profit(Q, d) ]

Choose Q* such that Expected Value(Q*) is maximized,
then validate robustness with sensitivity analysis.

Problem C – Profitability of a New Product

Monte Carlo simulation of per-unit profit under uncertain cost structure.

  • Objective: Estimate expected profit per unit and risk bounds for a new product before launch.
  • Methodology: Ran 20 Monte Carlo trials with random draws for purchase, labor, and transportation cost, holding demand at 1,000 units and computing per-unit profit each run.
  • Result: Mean profit per unit ≈ $6.90, standard deviation ≈ $2.27, with a 95% confidence interval between ~$4.46 and ~$9.34, supporting a tentative recommendation to launch.
For each trial i:
    purchase_costᵢ ~ Dist(...)
    labor_costᵢ    ~ Dist(...)
    transport_costᵢ~ Dist(...)
    profit_per_unitᵢ = SellingPrice
                       - purchase_costᵢ
                       - labor_costᵢ
                       - transport_costᵢ

Aggregate:
    μ = mean(profit_per_unitᵢ)
    σ = sd(profit_per_unitᵢ)
    95% CI ≈ μ ± 1.96·σ/√n

Problem D – Copier Selection for a Law Office

LP-based cost comparison between copier leasing options.

  • Objective: Choose between a regular copier and a high-speed copier by minimizing daily operating cost.
  • Methodology: Calculated hourly operating and labor costs and used linear programming in Excel Solver to compare total daily cost for required print volumes.
  • Result: The high-speed copier delivered a daily savings of ~$11.20 compared to the regular option, driven by shorter processing time and lower labor cost per page.
Minimize:   TotalDailyCost = LeaseCost_A + Wage·Hours_A
Subject to:  Throughput_A ≥ RequiredPages
            Hours_A ≥ 0

Solve for each copier option and select the lower TotalDailyCost.

Problem E – Patent Infringement Lawsuit Decision

Decision tree and EMV analysis for legal strategy (settle vs. trial).

  • Objective: Decide whether to settle a patent dispute or go to trial based on expected monetary value.
  • Methodology: Built a decision tree with branches for settlement and trial, estimating probabilities and payoffs for outcomes like win, lose, and partial settlement; then computed EMV for each branch.
  • Result: Going to trial yielded an EMV of ~$1.8M versus ~$1.5M for settling, leading to a recommendation to proceed with litigation (subject to non-financial risk considerations).
EMV(Settle) = P(settle) · Payoff_settle
EMV(Trial)   = P(win)·Payoff_win + P(lose)·Payoff_lose

Choose the option with higher EMV, then review legal risk qualitatively.

Problem F – Machine Assignment for Aircraft Part Manufacturing

Binary integer linear programming for machinist–machine assignment.

  • Objective: Assign four machinists to four machines (metal lathe, turning, milling, radial drill) to minimize total production time.
  • Methodology: Formulated a BILP in LINGO with decision variables xij indicating whether machinist i is assigned to machine j, including a certification constraint preventing Machinist 3 from operating the turning machine.
  • Result: Optimal assignment: Machinist 3 → Metal Lathe, Machinist 1 → Turning Machine, Machinist 2 → Milling Machine, Machinist 4 → Radial Drill, with a total processing time of 100 minutes.
Minimize   Σᵢ Σⱼ (timeᵢⱼ · xᵢⱼ)

Subject to:
    Σⱼ xᵢⱼ = 1      for each machinist i
    Σᵢ xᵢⱼ = 1      for each machine j
    x₃,turning = 0  -- Machinist 3 cannot use turning machine
    xᵢⱼ ∈ {0,1}

Usage Instructions

  • System Requirements: Excel with Solver Add-In enabled, LINGO installed on a standard workstation.
  • File Setup: Download the Excel workbooks and LINGO model files, then review problem-specific tabs and documentation.
  • Configuration: Adjust parameters (arrival rates, costs, demand, processing times) to match the organization’s data.
  • Execution: Run Solver in Excel for LP/queue/decision problems; execute BILP and MILP models in LINGO.
  • Verification: Cross-check outputs with hand calculations and perform sensitivity analysis around key assumptions.

Testing, Debugging & Future Enhancements

  • Validated constraints and model logic by adjusting parameters and confirming solution stability.
  • Ran sensitivity analysis on demand, cost, and probability inputs to test robustness.
  • Iteratively refined models when Solver/lingo results conflicted with manual expectations.
  • Identified opportunities to extend models with real-time data feeds and automated reporting.
  • Potential future work includes layering on machine learning for demand prediction and dynamic resource allocation.
