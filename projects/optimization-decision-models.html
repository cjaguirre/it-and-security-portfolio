Operations Management Optimization Models Simulation Decision Analysis

Quantitative Decision Modeling

A six part operations and supply chain portfolio that applies queueing theory, linear programming, mixed and binary integer programming, Monte Carlo simulation, and decision tree analysis to real business scenarios in retail, inventory management, product launch, legal strategy, and manufacturing. Each model moves from structured data and assumptions to a clear recommendation that supports cost control, service quality, and risk aware decision making.

Operations and Supply Chain Management · Fall 2023 Tools: Excel Solver, LINGO, Monte Carlo Simulation

Project Narrative

This project sits at the intersection of operations research and managerial decision making. Over the course of the semester, I worked through six structured business problems that mirror situations faced by managers in retail checkout lines, grocery inventory planning, new product launches, legal services, and manufacturing shop floors.

For each scenario, I translated a verbal description into a quantitative model, solved it using tools like Excel Solver and LINGO, and then interpreted the numbers in plain language so a non technical manager could act on the recommendation. The portfolio shows how math driven models directly support staffing levels, stocking policies, capital equipment choices, legal strategy, and assignment of skilled labor.

What I Built

  • Queueing based staffing recommendation for a retail checkout system to keep customer wait times within target.
  • Inventory decision framework using payoff and regret tables for a perishable specialty steak product.
  • Monte Carlo simulation that quantifies profit variability and risk for a new product launch.
  • Linear programming cost comparison model for choosing between copier alternatives in a law office.
  • Decision tree and expected monetary value analysis for a patent infringement lawsuit strategy.
  • Binary integer assignment model for pairing machinists with machines to minimize total processing time.

Project overview and analytical toolkit

The six problems are organized into three major categories of operations research: optimization models, simulation models, and decision models. Together they form a compact but comprehensive toolkit for reasoning about capacity, uncertainty, and tradeoffs in real business environments.

  • Optimization models: queueing based staffing decisions, linear programming cost comparison, and binary integer assignment for labor and machine pairing.
  • Simulation models: Monte Carlo sampling to understand the variability and downside risk of a new product profit per unit.
  • Decision models: payoff and regret tables for perishable goods stocking, and decision tree analysis with expected monetary value for legal strategy.
  • Software and tools: Excel with Solver Add In for LP, mixed integer and nonlinear optimization, and LINGO for compact mathematical model specification.

Optimization models — staffing, cost minimization, and assignment

Problem A — Customer checkout optimization with queueing theory

Using an M/M/c queue to balance staffing cost and customer waiting time.

A retail manager was concerned that customers were leaving the store without purchasing because the checkout line looked too long. The business question was simple but important: how many cashiers should be staffed so that a typical customer does not wait more than a few minutes, without overspending on labor.

  • Modeled customer arrivals as a Poisson process and service times as exponentially distributed.
  • Used an M/M/c queueing framework to estimate utilization, probability of waiting, and expected wait time.
  • Evaluated scenarios with one, two, and three cashiers and compared performance measures.
  • Interpreted results from a customer experience and labor cost perspective for a store manager.
ρ = λ / (c · μ)                 -- server utilization
Lq ≈ function(λ, μ, c)            -- expected number in queue
Wq = Lq / λ                        -- expected waiting time in queue
W  = Wq + 1 / μ                    -- total time in system

Choose the smallest c such that Wq stays under
the target waiting time (for example 5 minutes).

The analysis showed that a single cashier creates very high utilization and long queues, while two cashiers still leave many customers waiting beyond the target. With three cashiers, the model kept expected waiting time under the threshold and dramatically reduced the chance of visible line build up, at a labor cost increase that was justified by the improvement in service level.

Problem D — Linear programming cost comparison for copier selection

Modeling daily operating cost of copier options in a law office.

A law office needed to choose between a regular copier and a high speed copier. Each option had different lease payments, speeds, and operating costs. The firm wanted a structured comparison rather than relying on intuition about which copier felt faster.

  • Calculated effective cost per hour by combining lease and operating cost components.
  • Estimated the number of hours needed to process the required daily print volume under each copier.
  • Formulated total daily cost for each option as a simple linear programming style cost function.
  • Used Solver to confirm that the optimal decision is simply selecting the copier with lower total daily cost at the required throughput.
Let:
    V        = required pages per day
    rate_A   = pages per hour for Copier A
    rate_B   = pages per hour for Copier B
    cost_hrA = hourly operating plus lease cost for A
    cost_hrB = hourly operating plus lease cost for B

Then:
    hours_A        = V / rate_A
    hours_B        = V / rate_B
    TotalCost_A    = hours_A · cost_hrA
    TotalCost_B    = hours_B · cost_hrB

Choose the copier with the lower TotalCost value.

The high speed copier completed the required volume in substantially fewer hours, which reduced both direct operating cost and the implied labor time spent by staff waiting on prints. The resulting model showed an estimated daily savings of around eleven dollars compared to the regular copier, which compounds significantly over a long term lease.

Problem F — Binary integer assignment for machinist and machine pairing

Assigning machinists to machines to minimize total processing time.

A small manufacturing operation producing aircraft parts had four machinists and four machines. Each machinist had different processing times on each machine, and one machinist lacked certification on a specific machine type. The manager wanted the assignment of machinists to machines that minimized total time to complete a fixed batch of work.

  • Defined decision variables indicating whether machinist i was assigned to machine j.
  • Used an objective function that summed processing times across all machinist and machine pairs.
  • Enforced constraints that each machinist is assigned to exactly one machine and each machine is used by exactly one machinist.
  • Added a certification constraint that prevents an unqualified machinist from being assigned to the restricted machine.
  • Implemented and solved the model in LINGO as a classic assignment problem.
Minimize   Σᵢ Σⱼ ( time[i,j] · x[i,j] )

Subject to:
    Σⱼ x[i,j] = 1      for each machinist i
    Σᵢ x[i,j] = 1      for each machine j
    x[3,turning] = 0   machinist 3 not certified on turning machine
    x[i,j] ∈ {0,1}

The optimal assignment sent Machinist 3 to the metal lathe, Machinist 1 to the turning machine, Machinist 2 to the milling machine, and Machinist 4 to the radial drill. This combination produced a total processing time of 100 minutes, which was lower than any intuitive pairing the manager had considered and respected all skill constraints.

Simulation models — Monte Carlo profitability and risk analysis

Problem C — New product profitability under uncertain cost structure

Monte Carlo simulation for per unit profit and confidence intervals.

A firm was evaluating whether to introduce a new product. The selling price was known, but key cost drivers such as purchase cost, labor, and transportation varied from order to order. Instead of relying on a single best guess, the goal was to understand the distribution of profit per unit and the likelihood that the product would meet profitability targets.

  • Specified reasonable distributions for purchase cost, labor cost, and transportation cost based on past data.
  • Held demand at 1,000 units and calculated profit per unit for each trial.
  • Ran twenty simulation trials and summarized the mean, standard deviation, and confidence interval.
  • Interpreted the interval as a range of likely profit outcomes that management could plan around.
For each trial i:
    purchase_cost[i]   ~ Dist_purchase(...)
    labor_cost[i]      ~ Dist_labor(...)
    transport_cost[i]  ~ Dist_transport(...)

    profit_per_unit[i] = SellingPrice
                         - purchase_cost[i]
                         - labor_cost[i]
                         - transport_cost[i]

After n trials:
    μ  = mean(profit_per_unit)
    σ  = sd(profit_per_unit)
    CI = μ ± 1.96 · σ / √n   -- 95% confidence interval

The simulation produced an average profit of about six dollars and ninety cents per unit, with a standard deviation of roughly two dollars and twenty seven cents. The ninety five percent confidence interval for profit per unit ranged from roughly four and a half dollars to a little over nine dollars. This gave leadership a clear sense of both the upside and the downside, supporting a data informed decision to move forward while monitoring cost drivers closely.

Decision models — payoffs, regret, and legal strategy

Problem B — Specialty steak stocking with payoff and regret tables

Perishable inventory decision making under demand uncertainty.

A grocery manager needed to decide how many pounds of a specialty steak to stock each week. The meat is perishable, demand is uncertain, and unsold inventory must be marked down or discarded. Ordering too little leaves money on the table, while ordering too much wastes product and margin.

  • Defined several candidate order quantities and possible demand levels with probabilities.
  • Built a payoff table that computed weekly profit for each combination of order quantity and demand.
  • Derived a regret table to capture the opportunity loss of not choosing the best action in each demand state.
  • Applied expected value, optimistic, pessimistic, and minimax regret criteria.
  • Performed sensitivity analysis on demand probabilities to see when the recommended quantity would change.
Expected Profit(Q) = Σ over demand levels d [
                         P(D = d) · Profit(Q, d)
                     ]

Regret(Q, d) = BestProfit(d) - Profit(Q, d)

Choose:
    Q* that maximizes Expected Profit(Q)
    and performs well under minimax regret.

The analysis indicated that ordering about thirty five pounds of steak per week generated the highest expected profit, around seventy nine dollars, and remained robust under moderate changes in demand probabilities. This turned a gut feel stocking decision into a clearly supported policy.

Problem E — Patent infringement lawsuit strategy using decision trees

Expected monetary value comparison of settling versus going to trial.

A firm faced a patent infringement dispute and had two main options: accept a settlement offer or proceed to trial. Each path carried different probabilities of outcomes and very different financial consequences. Management wanted a structured way to compare the options and see which one created more expected value, before layering on subjective risk considerations.

  • Mapped the situation into a decision tree with a decision node (settle or trial) and chance nodes for possible outcomes.
  • Estimated probabilities of winning, losing, or achieving partial relief at trial.
  • Attached monetary consequences to each outcome, including legal costs and potential damages.
  • Calculated expected monetary value for both settling and going to trial.
  • Interpreted the numeric result alongside qualitative risk tolerance and reputational concerns.
EMV(Settle) = Payoff_settlement

EMV(Trial)  = P(win)   · Payoff_win
              + P(lose) · Payoff_lose
              + P(partial) · Payoff_partial

Recommend the option with the higher EMV,
then validate against the firm's risk appetite.

The decision tree showed that pursuing the lawsuit to trial produced an expected monetary value of roughly one point eight million dollars, compared to about one point five million dollars for accepting the settlement. While the trial option carried more variability, the structured analysis made it clear that, from a purely financial perspective, litigation was the better choice assuming the firm could tolerate the added risk.

Summary

This operations optimization and decision modeling portfolio shows how classical management science techniques move beyond textbook exercises into real business decisions. By combining queueing models, inventory analysis, Monte Carlo simulation, linear and integer programming, and decision trees, the work demonstrates a practical toolkit for choosing staffing levels, setting stocking policies, evaluating new products, selecting equipment, planning legal strategy, and assigning scarce skilled labor. The consistent pattern is taking messy, qualitative questions and turning them into structured, data driven recommendations that managers can confidently act on.
