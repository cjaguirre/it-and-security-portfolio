Operations Optimization & Decision Models

Linear programming • Monte Carlo simulation • Queuing models • Decision trees • Regret analysis • Binary integer programming

This project applies a full suite of operations research techniques to solve real business scenarios across logistics, risk modeling, resource allocation, capacity planning, and strategic decision-making. Each model focuses on improving efficiency, lowering cost, and supporting evidence-based decisions.

Methods include queue theory, payoff and regret tables, Monte Carlo simulation, linear programming (LP), decision tree modeling, and binary integer programming (BIP). These tools are widely used in analytics, supply chain, finance, and operations strategy roles.

Business Problems Solved

  • Minimizing labor cost while meeting production requirements
  • Reducing customer wait times through queue analysis
  • Optimizing resource allocation under constraints
  • Evaluating decisions under uncertainty using regrets/payoffs
  • Simulating 10,000+ random outcomes using Monte Carlo
  • Choosing optimal portfolio or operational decisions through decision trees
  • Solving binary integer problems for yes/no operational choices

Linear Programming (LP)

LP models were used to find the most cost-efficient combination of labor hours and materials while meeting minimum production and quality requirements. 

Maximize: Profit = 40x + 30y
Subject to:
    2x + y ≤ 100   -- Labor hours
    x + y ≤ 80     -- Machine capacity
    x, y ≥ 0

Solver results identified the production mix that maximizes profit while respecting constraints.

Decision Trees & Regret Analysis

Decision models evaluated uncertain outcomes like investment success, contractor performance, or weather-dependent operations. 

| Action         | Success | Failure |
|----------------|---------|---------|
| Invest         |  500k   | -200k   |
| Do Nothing     |   0     |   0     |

Expected Value:
EV = (0.6)(500k) + (0.4)(-200k) = 220k

The selected choice maximized expected value while minimizing regret across outcomes.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Each simulation ran thousands of randomized scenarios to forecast revenue, failure risk, or operational delays. Inputs were defined by probability distributions (normal, uniform, triangular). 

For i = 1 to 10,000:
    demand = Normal(mean=520, sd=40)
    revenue = demand * 35
    store results

Output included average results, probability of shortages, and risk intervals.

Binary Integer Programming (BIP)

BIP models solved “yes/no” decisions such as which projects to fund under budget constraints, or which equipment upgrades to schedule. 

Maximize: 12x1 + 10x2 + 8x3
Subject to:
    5x1 + 4x2 + 3x3 ≤ 10    -- Budget
    xi ∈ {0,1}
