Bellabeat Smart Device Usage Analysis

Used real Fitbit activity and sleep data to understand how wellness habits translate into smart device engagement. The analysis surfaces patterns Bellabeat can use to shape product features and targeted marketing for health conscious users.

Activity and sleep patterns Behavior and engagement insights

Project narrative

This project explores how people actually use their wellness wearables across a typical month. By combining daily steps, calories burned, active minutes, and sleep data from Fitbit, I built a picture of when users move, rest, and engage with their devices.

The goal is to give Bellabeat a data backed view of user behavior that can inform campaigns, in app nudges, and product decisions aimed at keeping users active, rested, and engaged with the Bellabeat ecosystem.

What I built

  • End to end R workflow to import, clean, and combine multiple Fitbit tables.
  • Feature set capturing daily activity, intensity, and sleep behavior.
  • Summary views that compare high and low engagement users.
  • Visuals that highlight weekday and weekend wellness patterns.
  • Actionable recommendations tied to Bellabeat marketing and product strategy.

Lab breakdown

1. Dataset and business question

Fitbit data used as a proxy for Bellabeat users

The analysis uses a public Fitbit dataset with about 30 users over 30 days. It includes daily steps, distance, calories, intensity, and separate tables for sleep and weight. The guiding question is how these patterns can help Bellabeat increase activation, retention, and daily engagement with its own smart devices.

  • Daily activity metrics: steps, distance, calories, intensity minutes.
  • Sleep metrics where available: total minutes and time in bed.
  • User level identifiers to track behavior over time.
  • Thirty day window aligned with a typical onboarding period.

2. Data cleaning and feature engineering

Joined activity and sleep tables and created daily wellness signals

Activity and sleep tables did not share a perfect key out of the box. I standardized date formats, removed duplicate entries, and then joined tables at the user and date level. From there, I engineered features that describe a typical day for each user.

  • Converted all date fields to a consistent format.
  • Joined daily activity and sleep data on user and date.
  • Computed total active minutes by combining activity intensity fields.
  • Derived day of week for weekday versus weekend comparisons.
# Merge and engineer core daily metrics
daily_activity <- read_csv("dailyActivity_merged.csv") %>%
  clean_names()

daily_sleep <- read_csv("sleepDay_merged.csv") %>%
  clean_names()

daily_activity <- daily_activity %>%
  mutate(activity_date = mdy(activity_date))

daily_sleep <- daily_sleep %>%
  mutate(sleep_date = mdy(sleep_day))

merged <- daily_activity %>%
  inner_join(daily_sleep,
             by = c("id", "activity_date" = "sleep_date")) %>%
  mutate(
    day_of_week = wday(activity_date, label = TRUE),
    active_minutes_total = very_active_minutes +
                           fairly_active_minutes +
                           lightly_active_minutes
  )

3. Daily and weekly behavior patterns

Compared movement and rest behavior across the week

With a clean daily view of each user, I examined how steps, active minutes, calories, and sleep changed by day of week. This highlights when users are most engaged with their wellness routine and where there may be drop off.

  • Steps and calories tend to peak on weekdays, especially Tuesday through Thursday.
  • Sleep duration clusters in the six to seven and a half hour range for most users.
  • Active minutes align with workdays, suggesting commute or gym patterns.
  • Weekends show lower movement for many users, even when sleep time increases slightly.

4. Engagement and marketing opportunities

Mapped behavior patterns to specific Bellabeat actions

Once patterns were clear, I translated them into concrete actions Bellabeat could take. The focus is on using data to time nudges, adjust messaging, and design challenges that align with how users already live their lives.

  • Use weekday streaks and step goals to reinforce existing activity habits.
  • Introduce gentle weekend reminders aimed at maintaining momentum rather than pushing intense workouts.
  • Highlight sleep insights in the app for users consistently below recommended rest levels.
  • Promote long term wellness programs that track both movement and sleep quality together.

# Summarize key wellness metrics by day of week
day_summary <- merged %>%
  group_by(day_of_week) %>%
  summarise(
    avg_steps = mean(total_steps, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_calories = mean(calories, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_active_minutes = mean(active_minutes_total, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_sleep_minutes = mean(total_minutes_asleep, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  arrange(day_of_week)

Architecture overview

Components

  • Fitbit source tables — daily activity, sleep, and calories.
  • R processing layer — tidyverse pipeline for cleaning and joins.
  • Feature store — engineered daily metrics and weekday labels.
  • Visualization layer — R charts and optional Tableau dashboards.
  • Insight and strategy layer — marketing and product recommendations.

Data flow

  • Raw Fitbit CSVs → R import and cleaning.
  • Cleaned activity and sleep data → joined daily view.
  • Daily view → aggregated by user and day of week.
  • Aggregations → charts and dashboard views.
  • Visuals and tables → Bellabeat engagement strategy and campaigns.

Figure 8 — Composite dashboard that brings together activity, sleep, and calorie insights for Bellabeat stakeholders.

Key insights

  • Most users fall short of eight hours of sleep even on low activity days.
  • Weekday activity is more structured, which makes it easier to anchor challenges and streaks.
  • Weekend engagement dips suggest a need for lighter, lifestyle oriented nudges instead of strict goals.
  • Users with more consistent sleep and activity patterns tend to show more stable device usage.
