What I built

An R pipeline that cleans and joins Fitbit daily activity and sleep tables, engineers features for weekday segmentation, and produces summary charts that show when users move, rest, and engage most with their devices — then translates those patterns into Bellabeat marketing and product strategy.

Key outcomes

  • • Activity and sleep tables joined on user + date into one daily view
  • • active_minutes_total and day_of_week features engineered
  • • Weekday activity peaks and weekend dips clearly identified
  • • Most users sleep below 7.5 hrs — strong case for sleep coaching features
  • • Engagement strategy tied directly to observed behavioral patterns

Lab Walkthrough

Cleaning & Feature Engineering

The activity and sleep tables don't share a perfect key out of the box — date formats differ and some users appear in one table but not the other. After standardizing formats and removing zero-step rows, the tables are joined at the user + date grain. Two features make the behavioral analysis possible: active_minutes_total (all intensity tiers combined) and day_of_week for weekday segmentation.

Merge & engineer code

library(tidyverse)
library(lubridate)
library(janitor)

daily_activity <- read_csv(
  "dailyActivity_merged.csv"
) %>% clean_names()

merged <- daily_activity %>%
  inner_join(daily_sleep,
    by = c("id", "activity_date")) %>%
  mutate(
    day_of_week = wday(
      activity_date, label = TRUE),
    active_minutes_total =
      very_active_minutes +
      fairly_active_minutes +
      lightly_active_minutes
  )

Behavior Patterns

Three patterns stand out from the data and each one maps directly to a Bellabeat engagement opportunity. Weekday activity is structured and consistent. Weekend activity drops. And most users consistently fall short of the recommended 8 hours of sleep.

Movement by weekday

  • • Average steps exceed 8,000 on several weekdays
  • • Tuesday and Saturday show calorie peaks; Thursday dips
  • • Midweek highs are natural anchors for step challenges and streaks
  • • Weekend dip suggests gentler nudges rather than aggressive goals

Average daily steps by weekday — weekdays outpace weekends, especially midweek

Sleep deficit

  • • Most users sleep 6–7.5 hrs — below the recommended 8
  • • Short sleepers are a high-impact audience for sleep coaching content
  • • The gap between actual and recommended sleep is a clear product hook

Summary code — metrics by weekday

day_summary <- merged %>%
  group_by(day_of_week) %>%
  summarise(
    avg_steps         = mean(total_steps, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_calories      = mean(calories, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_active_mins   = mean(active_minutes_total),
    avg_sleep_minutes = mean(total_minutes_asleep),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  arrange(day_of_week)

Engagement Strategy

Tier 1 — Activity Engagement

Leverage structured weekday routines to build lasting habits.

  • Weekday streak goalsReinforce midweek activity peaks with step challenges and rewards timed to when users are already in motion.
  • Calorie burn insightsShow the direct relationship between movement and energy expenditure — the data backs "move more, burn more."
  • Tiered active minute goalsSet goals that align with natural weekday patterns rather than pushing uniform daily targets.

Tier 2 — Sleep Optimization

Position sleep tracking as a core value add for wellness users.

  • Sleep coaching contentTarget users consistently below 7 hours with personalized tips — this is most of the sample.
  • Bedtime remindersGentle nudges based on individual sleep patterns, not a one-size-fits-all schedule.
  • Show the connectionCorrelate better sleep with improved activity metrics so users see why sleep matters to their goals.

Tier 3 — Weekend Retention

Maintain engagement during unstructured weekend periods.

  • Lighter weekend targetsAdjust goals downward on weekends rather than flagging lower activity as a failure.
  • Recognize lifestyle activityTrack and reward non-gym activities like hiking or walking — weekend behavior that doesn't show up as steps.
  • Streak maintenance focusWeekend messaging should be about keeping streaks alive, not pushing intense workouts.

What I Learned

Key findings

  • • Weekday activity is more consistent and structured — natural anchors for challenges and streaks
  • • Most users sleep below recommended levels — sleep coaching is a clear, data-backed product opportunity
  • • Active minutes and calories show strong positive correlation, validating "move more" as messaging
  • • Weekend engagement dips suggest the app needs a different tone and goal structure on weekends
  • • Joining activity and sleep data reveals patterns neither table shows on its own
